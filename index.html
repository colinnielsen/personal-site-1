Dr. Strangecode or: How I Learned to Stop Worrying and Love the Process

A reflection on my learning experience at Galvanize

Feb 27, 2018

Set the Stage

For this project I set the stage dividing my work space into 2/3 1/3 with atom and firefox and made sure my notifications were off! Here's a vid https://youtu.be/pQLbhPaG9kU

Reflection

I began Galvanize chomping at the bit really just ready to learn and soak in absolutely all that I could. Last week began well as we all worked quickly through cli drills and were, for the most part, at the same level of knowledge. By the end of the week I already felt myself falling behind in comparison to the rest of my classmates. So naturally, the stress set in, followed by harsh self-analysis and frustration. I worked hard to catch-up, and if I'm honest, I was working fast, not to necessarily learn any of the complicated content. All of this "work" peaked for me this Monday. I worked so hard at CSS to format my first page - and by hard I mean to literally throw my head over and over again into the problem. Today is when I decided to change the way I was approaching the course and how to learn. I decided that I needed to prioritize process over product and see what I could learn...and it went great!

Today was my most productive day. It left me with a sense of accomplishment with brainpower still left to spare at the end of the day. I definitely was open to more information using a slower, more methodical pacing to my SMART goal day plan.

One thing that helped me the most today, especially in learning more CSS, was to play with the code. After a very constructive breakout with Brooks, I came back to working on my website with the devtools on Firefox. This helped me really play around with the css right in the console and to get a deeper understanding of what values I could use and how my webpage is set up.

In summation, today I learned to work smarter, not harder.

Recently, I've been struggling with understanding Javascript. Ive been trying to really understand the logic behind how it works and why it does things, and honestly, it's been very difficult. Smart Cities & Open Data

People I Met

- Demetri - He works in digital marketing at a company called Professional Advantage. - https://www.linkedin.com/in/demetriosdemetriou/

Andrew - He's a full stack dev who works mostly with Python and JS. - https://www.linkedin.com/in/andrew-kolansky-58031472/

What I Learned

The talk was on the proliferation of smart cities and how tech is being integrated into city life. We had a speaker who was a counselwoman for Westminster where they've actually been pioneering a lot of different technologies like smart light poles and smart crosswalks. We also talked a lot about city planning and archetecture fields utalizing augmented reality technologies, which I found especially interesting. The speakers talked on the specific applications of integrating the "internet of things" into the citizen's environment to promote safety, ease of use, or community participation. I learned a lot about the future of Denver and tech, and also about data.gov from the second speaker who was a leader of the Colorado Smart Cities Alliance. Data.gov is a transparency project hosted by the US government that publicizes data it collects about things like climate, public health, or education. Cities also push their data --anything from traffic stats and live-update bus locations to pot-hole repair times-- to the public domain for us all to access.

I asked some questions about how open data could be implement in mobile or web apps for citizens and the speaker said he sees a future coming very soon where start-ups work with state and local government to push technology into the citysphere.

  • What are some more practical applications of the government's open data working with the tech market?
  • How do you think that augmented reality technology could affect the average citizen's life in 5-7 years?
